# The Try Girl — Article Writing Style Guide

Derived from published Dance Chat articles on thetrygirl.substack.com (Andy Chiang, Eskillz, Una episodes).

## Voice & Tone
- **Intimate, unhurried, almost cinematic.** The writing moves like a slow pan — it lingers on moments before cutting to the next scene. Never rushed, never listicle-like.
- **The writer is present but invisible.** No first-person. No "I sat across from..." No editorial opinion. The author sets scenes with specificity, bridges themes with short observational sentences, then gets out of the way so the subject can speak.
- **Poetic restraint with punch.** Short declarative sentences create rhythm between longer paragraphs. "And yet, he was the child who danced in secret." "But the dancing didn't stop." These land because they're earned by the buildup around them.

## Structure
- **Open with a sensory scene or a striking image** — not a bio, not a summary. Drop the reader into a physical reality.
  - Andy: "Lights turn on and off, music plays on repeat. The tempo is fast, unrelenting. Sweat streams down spines, legs begin to shake..."
  - Eskillz: "The first time Eskillz realized that the body could be used did not happen in a dance studio, but in a dojo."
  - Una: contrast hook — "17-year-old Una is often the youngest in the room."
- **Thematic sections with evocative headers** that work like chapter titles:
  - "The kid who danced in secret"
  - "A Cultural Ambassador from Taiwan to Broadway"
  - "He Doesn't Really Believe in the Borders Between Styles"
  - "Space, Responsibility, and Kindness"
- **3-6 thematic sections**, each a self-contained vignette with its own emotional arc. NOT chronological — organized by theme and feeling.
- **Close with philosophy, not biography.** The ending expands outward from the personal to the universal. Let the subject's worldview resonate as the final note:
  - Andy: "To give the world richer experiences. And that is the meaning of art."
  - Eskillz: "You already know how to move. Everyone can dance."

## Emotional Architecture
- **Vulnerability is structural, not decorative.** Every article has at least one section where the subject reveals doubt, fear, loss, or struggle — not as a problem they overcame, but as ongoing texture in their life.
  - Andy: the rupture of Nai-Ni's death — "For the company, it was an almost fatal rupture."
  - Eskillz: "It took him a long time to finally say he was a good dancer — because he had to stop beating himself up for imperfection."
- **Contrast creates emotional resonance.** Juxtapose opposites within scenes:
  - MIT engineer running a dance company
  - The child who danced in secret in a family where everyone danced
  - Teaching again two weeks after giving birth
- **Specificity over abstraction.** Not "she struggled financially" but "two classes every Saturday — that's all her allowance could cover." Not "he trained in many styles" but "he watched popping and animation videos on YouTube, practicing Jersey Club rhythms, stops, and accents."

## Technique
- **Scene-setting through physical detail** — sweat, tempo, shaking legs, cramped rooms, specific street corners. Ground every section in a body or a place.
- **Direct quotes as emotional anchors.** Every major emotional beat is delivered in the subject's own voice, not paraphrased. Quotes are chosen for feeling, not information.
- **Transitional sentences are observational, not connective.** Instead of "Moving on to discuss..." use a short observation: "He has little interest in ranking dancers." "Dance is no longer just work for him."
- **Let paradox breathe.** When the subject says something contradictory or surprising, don't explain it. Let it sit. The reader will feel the tension.
- **Section endings land on resonance, not summary.** End sections on a quote or an image, not a wrap-up sentence.

## What NOT to Do
- Don't write chronological biography. The reader should feel themes, not a timeline.
- Don't open with "[Name] is a [profession] from [place]" — that's a press release.
- Don't summarize the interview. Transform raw Q&A into narrative prose.
- Don't resolve every struggle into triumph. Real lives are messier.
- Don't editorialize ("she is truly an inspiration"). Show, never tell.
- Don't use generic inspirational closings. The subject's own philosophy is the closing.
- Don't explain quotes — let them breathe. If a quote needs a paragraph of context to make sense, choose a different quote.
- Don't use transition words (however, moreover, additionally, furthermore). Cut between scenes cinematically.

## Bilingual Note
The Chinese version should NOT be a translation. It should be a culturally adapted retelling — different metaphors, different cultural references, phrasing natural to Chinese readership. The emotional architecture stays the same; the language and cultural texture should feel native.

## Length & Format
- 800–1200 words per version
- English byline: `By The Try Girl | Dance Chat`
- Chinese byline: `作者：The Try Girl | 舞所不谈`
- Markdown format with `##` section headers
- End with a `---` divider and social links / follow section
